Research

“The key to prediction and understanding lies in the elucidation of mechanisms underlying observed patterns. Typically, these mechanisms operate at different scales than those on which the patterns are observed.” — Levin (1992), The Problem of Pattern and Scale in Ecology, Ecology 73(6): 1943–1967

We work at the interface of plant science, ecology, Earth system science, and remote sensing. We aim to improve predictive understanding of our diverse and heterogeneous biosphere, leveraging novel ecological data sets, advanced computational tools, and theoretical analysis. Our research addresses classic questions across ecological scales: To what extent do traits determine plant performance and vital rates? What are the key dimensions of plant functional diversity? How do biodiversity and environmental heterogeneity interact to determine large-scale processes such as carbon and water cycles? We also develop novel quantitative methods to extract ecologically relevant information from multi-platform remote sensing products, particularly terrestrial lidar data.

What we do

🌳 Ecosystem modeling

Process-based models (the ED2 lineage and beyond) that scale plant physiology up to ecosystem and biosphere dynamics.

🛰️ Ecological remote sensing

Lidar, drones, and satellite products to characterize vegetation dynamics and land surface processes across spatial and temporal scales.

💻 Model–data integration

A ModEx approach that fuses field experiments, observations, and models to improve predictions and generate new hypotheses about ecosystem dynamics.

Research Projects

Current lab research revolves around Tropical forest resilience under environmental change, Forest biomass monitoring and modeling, and Mapping ponds, wetland and their ecosystem functioning supported by NSF, DOE, USDA, NY DEC, NASA, Cornell CALS, and private foundations.
